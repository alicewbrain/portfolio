Designing for Complexity: A Trilogy

#1 Complexity Management

The hardest part of design is often invisible.

Real design challenges are about structure, boundaries, and how a system is understood. As products grow, complexity becomes almost unavoidable. But human understanding does not scale at the same speed as the system itself.

We rarely understand a complex system all at once. Most of the time, understanding is built piece by piece. Cognitive psychologist George A. Miller once suggested that human working memory can only handle a limited amount of information at the same time.

This is how I think about product structure: a system should not require people to track too many dependent conditions at once.

Language itself works this way. Sounds become letters. Letters become words. Words become sentences.

Interfaces work in a similar way. People do not instantly understand an entire interface. They first understand smaller parts, then slowly build a mental model of the whole system.

Navigation software is a good example.

Before navigation starts, the full map is difficult to understand. You see dense roads, highways, directions, and distances all at once. But once navigation begins, the system no longer asks you to understand the entire city.

Instead, it tells you:

  • Turn left in 200 meters
  • Go straight
  • Enter the highway
  • Keep right

The complexity did not disappear. It was reorganized.

The system transforms one massive problem into a series of smaller, manageable tasks. What people truly understand is often not the whole system itself, but how smaller parts gradually form the whole.

This is why, when designing complex systems, I focus less on features themselves and more on how changes spread through the system.

A new feature never affects just one place. It creates new dependencies, new interactions, and new cognitive costs inside the existing structure.

When boundaries are unclear, even a small local change can force users to relearn the entire system. I see this as a signal: when a local change starts affecting unrelated areas, the system structure is under pressure.

I often use one question to evaluate product structure:

When one part changes, do people need to rebuild their understanding of the whole system?

If the answer is yes, the system is usually too tightly coupled.
If the answer is no, understanding can still grow progressively from local parts.

This is not an absolute rule. It is simply a pattern I have repeatedly observed while working on complex products.

Many people think design is about making things simple. But I believe good design rarely removes complexity completely.

Most of the time, design is about managing complexity. Helping people understand, act, and gradually build confidence in a system within the limits of human cognition.

Good structure does not eliminate complexity. It keeps complexity within a range humans can still understand.

Systems can keep becoming more complex.
People cannot.

#2 Human Interface

Systems can be complex, but interfaces must be simple.

Product interfaces should align with user intuition. However, complexity is the norm in certain industries and is often taken for granted. The underlying layers of these products usually involve vast amounts of states, rules, and technical constraints. While logical to engineers, they remain incomprehensible to most users.

Current AI products, for example, are in a phase of powerful yet chaotic infancy. Their underlying capabilities are astonishing, but the steep learning curve for deep usage limits widespread adoption. From a human perspective, this is not a true user experience. What users actually want to do is simple, yet the product forces them to understand how the entire system works first. This is not unprecedented; many complex products suffer from the same issue. Eventually, people accept this complexity as a given, as if the system is naturally meant to be complicated and users must bear the burden. In reality, an overly complex system is merely a reflection of current engineering boundaries.

According to Tesler's Law, every system has an inherent amount of complexity that cannot be removed. The common belief is that design makes things simpler. In reality, design rarely reduces complexity — the system remains complex. What actually changes is how that complexity is redistributed. Good design should find the sweet spot between technical backends and user cognitive loads, ensuring that complexity resides where it is best handled.

To achieve this goal, I believe we must maintain two bottom lines:

Hide unnecessary processes.
Under the premise of balancing design and backend technical details, do not force users to manage cognitive loads irrelevant to their intent the moment they enter.

Retain critical decision points.
Intent-driven design is by no means a black box. Designers must precisely distinguish between superfluous complexity and essential complexity.

In DeFi, for example, while "Approve" is a piece of system language, it represents a critical risk boundary. Good design simplifies operations while ensuring users retain final control over risks, such as slippage, security, and asset authorization. The task of design is to provide progressive disclosure. There is a delicate balance in pursuing intent-driven design: we must not strip away the user's right to know for the sake of extreme simplicity. Those seemingly complex pieces of information are vital foundations for user decision-making.

The Moment Design Drives Mass Adoption

More than ever, humans need to occupy the decision-making layer, while the system acts as the supporting layer. The defining moments of design are more critical than before. Through an elegant abstraction layer, complex parameters are transformed into intuitive interactions. If a user encounters a failure, the interface must offer transparency and explainability, helping them understand the reason behind the error instead of trapping them in the distrust of a technical black box. A designer's job is not just drawing UIs. Only when designers build better abstraction layers to turn powerful features into intuitive actions can the adoption of AI truly scale.

Products should no longer expose complexity; they must establish an interaction model that aligns more closely with human thought.

Enable people to take action based on their intent, without requiring them to become engineers first.

#3 Moving Complexity

Good Design Doesn't Make Complexity Vanish

In reality, complexity can never truly be eliminated from highly complex systems. This holds true for AI systems, developer tools, fintech, and blockchain platforms.

The real question is: who should bear the burden of this complexity?

Some products keep complexity inside the system, while others expose it directly to the user. The difference between these two approaches defines the product experience. Some products boast extensive features, powerful capabilities, and cutting-edge systems, yet using them feels exhausting. This exhaustion does not stem from a surplus of features, but because the product dumps its systemic burden straight onto the user.

Users are forced to:

  • Comprehend workflows
  • Manage states
  • Remember dependencies
  • Predict system behavior
  • Handle exceptions

This complexity, which rightfully belongs to the system, is shifted onto human cognition. Humans are not wired to sustain a high cognitive load for long periods. This issue has become glaringly obvious in the world of AI agents. You might think a multi-agent system represents a higher, more efficient tier of technology. In truth, it often just converts problems that should have been solved, or problems that should not have existed in the first place, into higher token consumption.

The system appears more efficient, yet users find themselves:

  • Debugging agents
  • Managing context
  • Correcting hallucinations
  • Maintaining workflows
  • Understanding dependencies between agents

Design in the Era of Hyper Complexity Reclaiming Human Agency Amidst Systemic Complexity

A good solution never forces humans to adapt to the tool. Otherwise, what we ultimately master is not how to solve the problem, but merely how to maintain the system. When a tool begins demanding human adaptation, and when the migration of complexity fails, the system's capabilities begin to outpace the limits of human understanding.

Design does not make a system simple. What truly matters is how we position complexity, ensuring that people are not forced to bear it all at once. Good design is not just what it looks like on the surface; it functions like a structure. A sound structure allows complexity to coexist so that the components can be intricate and the system massive, yet the whole remains entirely comprehensible.

The true value of design lies in building a functioning interface between human cognition and system complexity. As the world becomes increasingly virtual, design that still enables people to understand, act, and genuinely feel becomes all the more precious.
